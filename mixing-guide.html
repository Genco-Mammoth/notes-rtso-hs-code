Mixing & Blending Guide: EU Classification of Mixed Oils

GIR Rules 2(b), 3(a)–(c) CJEU Case C-233/15 Practical strategies

How the EU classifies mixtures of vegetable oils under the Combined Nomenclature, what the General Interpretative Rules say about blends, and whether deliberately mixing oils can change the tariff classification — with practical guidance for rubber seed oil blends.

1. The Legal Framework: How the EU Classifies Mixtures

When two or more vegetable oils are blended, the EU applies a strict hierarchy of General Interpretative Rules (GIRs) to determine the correct heading. These rules are part of the Harmonized System and are binding on all WTO members.

1
GIR 1: Classify by heading terms and notes
First, check if a specific heading covers the mixture. Headings 1517 (edible mixtures) and 1518 (inedible mixtures) exist specifically for oil blends. If the blend is edible → 1517. If inedible/industrial → 1518.
↓
2
GIR 2(b): Does the addition change the product's character?
A heading for a single material includes mixtures with small amounts of other substances only if the addition does not deprive the goods of the character of the named material. Trace impurities from shared equipment? Stays as single oil. Deliberate 5% blend? Likely becomes a mixture.
↓
3
GIR 3(a): Most specific description
If GIR 1 and 2(b) don't resolve it, check if one heading is more specific. For oil blends, both component headings usually describe only part of the mixture → equally specific → GIR 3(a) fails.
↓
4
GIR 3(b): Essential character
The mixture is classified by the component that gives it its essential character. For oils, this is usually the dominant component by weight/volume. A 70/30 blend → classified by the 70% oil. A 50/50 blend → GIR 3(b) may fail.
↓
5
GIR 3(c): Last heading in numerical order
If essential character cannot be determined (roughly equal components), classify under the heading occurring last in numerical order. E.g., a 50/50 blend of 1514 oil and 1515 oil → heading 1515.

2. Edible vs. Inedible: The Critical Distinction

The single most important factor for oil blend classification is whether the mixture is edible or inedible. This was confirmed by the CJEU in Case C-233/15 (28 April 2016):

CJEU Case C-233/15
88% rapeseed oil + 12% sunflower oil blend

The Court ruled that all objective characteristics and properties must be considered, and the intended use (edible vs. inedible) is the determining factor. The producer's own declarations about the production process (e.g., that noxious substances could not be excluded) were sufficient to classify the mixture as inedible under 1518 00 31, even though laboratory analysis did not detect noxious substances.

3. Can You Blend Oils to Change Classification?

This is the key strategic question. The short answer: yes, if the blend is genuine and materially changes the product's character. No, if it's a token addition designed solely for tariff engineering.

Legitimate blending
When a blend changes classification
  • Substantial proportion: Both oils are present in meaningful quantities (e.g., 60/40 or 50/50)
  • Genuine commercial purpose: The blend has a real industrial reason (e.g., optimising FAME yield, adjusting viscosity, meeting a fuel spec)
  • Physically blended before import: The mixture exists as a single product at the time of import, not combined on paper only
  • Documented: Blend composition, production records, and buyer specifications all align
Risky / prohibited
When blending will be challenged
  • Token addition: Adding 1–3% of a second oil just to claim "mixture" status
  • No commercial purpose: The blend serves no functional purpose beyond tariff reduction
  • Paper-only blend: Two oils shipped separately but invoiced as a "blend"
  • Inconsistent documentation: Blend ratio varies without explanation, or buyer specs don't mention the second oil
GIR 2(b) sets a high bar
A heading for a single material includes mixtures with small additions only if the addition does not deprive the goods of the character of the named material. A 2% addition of jatropha oil to rubber seed oil almost certainly does not deprive the product of its character as rubber seed oil. Customs would classify it as 1515 90 59 (pure RSO), not 1518 00 31 (mixture).

4. Is There a De Minimis Threshold?

There is no formal percentage threshold in the HS or CN that defines when a minor addition transforms a single oil into a mixture. The test is factual, not numerical:

Scenario Likely classification Why
RSO with 0.5% residual hexane from extraction 1515 90 (single oil) Trace processing residue — does not alter character
RSO with 2% jatropha oil from shared equipment 1515 90 (single oil) Cross-contamination, not deliberate blend
RSO with 5% deliberately added jatropha oil Disputed — likely 1515 90 Low proportion; RSO character retained (GIR 2(b))
RSO with 20% jatropha oil 1518 00 31 (mixture) — if inedible/industrial Meaningful second component; character as blend
RSO 50% + jatropha 50% 1518 00 31 (mixture) Equal components — clearly a mixture
RSO 95% + jatropha 5% (token addition) 1515 90 (single oil) — risky challenge Token addition; customs will likely reject mixture claim
⚠ No bright-line rule

The lack of a formal threshold means every case is determined on its facts. The safest approach for legitimate blending is to use at least 20% of the second component, document the commercial rationale, and obtain a BTI ruling before import.

Customs authorities routinely challenge blends below 10% of the minor component. They almost never challenge blends above 30%.

5. Blending Strategies for Rubber Seed Oil

If the goal is to import rubber seed oil at the lowest possible duty, blending offers one potential path — but it must be done correctly:

Strategy Blend composition Target code Effective rate Risk level
A: Pure RSO, industrial 100% RSO 1515 90 40 3.2% Low — correct classification
B: RSO+jatropha blend 50% RSO + 50% jatropha, crude, industrial 1518 00 31 0% Moderate — needs BTI, genuine blend
C: RSO+neem blend 60% RSO + 40% neem, crude, industrial 1518 00 31 0% Moderate — both inedible, genuine
D: RSO token blend 95% RSO + 5% jatropha Claimed: 1518 00 31 0% claimed High — will be challenged
Strategy B/C vs Strategy A: Cost-benefit
A 50/50 RSO+jatropha blend at 0% (Strategy B) saves 3.2% vs. pure RSO at 3.2% (Strategy A). On 1,000 tonnes at €800/t CIF, that is €25,600 in duty savings. However, the blending itself has costs (sourcing, mixing, testing, documentation), and the BTI process takes 4–6 months. The strategy only makes sense for larger import volumes (>500 t/year).
EU buyer's perspective
Can European biodiesel plants accept mixed feedstock?

Yes. Most EU biodiesel plants are multi-feedstock facilities that process whatever oils are available at the best price. A blend of RSO + jatropha or RSO + neem would be technically acceptable for transesterification. However, the buyer must be aware of and agree to the blend composition, and the feedstock must meet the plant's technical specifications (FFA limits, phosphorus content, moisture). The buyer's purchase order should reference the blend ratio and CN code.

Anti-abuse warning
EU customs law (Union Customs Code) and CJEU case law recognise principles against abusive practices. If customs determines that blending was done solely to obtain a more favorable tariff treatment and the blend does not meaningfully change the product's character, they can reject the mixture classification and reclassify to the original single-oil heading — with back-duties and penalties.
