Indonesia's Three-Phase Preference Trajectory

Indonesia's relationship with EU trade preferences has evolved through three distinct phases. Understanding each phase — and the legal instruments behind it — is critical for planning.

Pre-2020 — Full GSP beneficiary
Indonesia enjoyed Standard GSP on eligible products
Indonesia was a Standard GSP beneficiary under Regulation (EU) No 978/2012. For non-graduated sections, Indonesian exports received either 0% (non-sensitive products) or a 3.5 percentage-point reduction on the MFN rate (sensitive products).
1 January 2020 — Section 3 graduated
GSP preferences suspended for Animal & Vegetable Oils
Under Article 8 of Regulation 978/2012, Indonesia's imports of Section 3 products exceeded the 15% graduation threshold over three consecutive years. The Commission suspended GSP preferences for Section 3 from Indonesia effective 1 January 2020.

Impact: HS 1515.90 products from Indonesia became subject to the full MFN rate. GSP preferences for other sections (e.g., textiles, footwear) continued.
September 2025 — CEPA negotiations concluded
EU-Indonesia Comprehensive Economic Partnership Agreement (CEPA)
Negotiations for the EU-Indonesia CEPA were concluded in September 2025 after more than a decade of talks (launched July 2013, 16 rounds of negotiations). The agreement, when ratified and in force, would progressively eliminate or reduce tariffs on most goods traded between the EU and Indonesia.

Status as of 4 July 2026: Not yet in force. The agreement must undergo legal revision, signature, and ratification by both the EU (Council decision, European Parliament consent) and Indonesia. Until entry into force, Indonesian exports pay full MFN rates.
1 January 2027 — Full GSP graduation
Indonesia graduates entirely from the EU GSP
The World Bank classified Indonesia as an upper-middle income country for three consecutive years (2024, 2025, 2026). Under Article 4(1)(a) of Regulation 978/2012, this triggers automatic graduation from the entire GSP scheme. The Commission adopted Delegated Regulation (EU) 2025/1951 removing Indonesia from the GSP beneficiary list effective 1 January 2027.

Impact: From this date, all Indonesian exports to the EU (not just Section 3) lose GSP preferences. Unless the CEPA enters into force by then, Indonesian exports face full MFN rates across the board.

The graduation of Indonesia's Section 3 preferences was an automatic legal mechanism, not a political decision. Here is the exact process by which it was triggered, with the legal basis at each step:

Indonesia context
Why Section 3 was graduated

Indonesia is the world's largest producer of palm oil, and palm oil derivatives dominate its exports to the EU under Section 3. Even though heading 1515 90 (rubber seed oil, shea butter, etc.) is a minor part of Section 3, graduation applies to the entire section — all CN codes in Chapters 15 lose GSP preferences simultaneously. There is no product-by-product graduation within a section.

Can graduated preferences be restored?
Yes — under Article 8(3), the graduation list is reviewed every three years. If a country's import share falls below the threshold, preferences can be re-established. However, for Indonesia's Section 3 this is unlikely in the foreseeable future. Moreover, Indonesia will graduate entirely from the GSP on 1 January 2027, making Section 3 graduation moot.

Important timing risk
Indonesia graduates from the GSP entirely on 1 January 2027. If the CEPA has not entered into force by that date, there will be a preference gap period where Indonesia has neither GSP preferences nor FTA preferences — paying full MFN rates on all products, not just Section 3.

Strategic implication
Vietnamese and Singaporean exporters of the same vegetable oils pay 0% duty, while Indonesian exporters pay 6.4%–9.6%. This creates a 6–10 percentage point cost disadvantage at the EU border for Indonesian-origin goods in this category.

Practical Guidance for Indonesian Exporters

Certificate of origin

No preference certificate is applicable. Indonesian exporters should declare the goods under 1515 90 59 or 1515 90 99 and expect the MFN rate to be applied. No Form A (GSP) or EUR.1 document will provide a reduction.

Classification strategy

Exporters of crude oils should ensure correct classification under 1515 90 59 (6.4%) rather than 1515 90 99 (9.6%). The distinction hinges on whether the oil is crude/unrefined vs. refined. Proper documentation of processing state is essential.

End-use exemptions

If the oils are intended for incorporation into ships, vessels, or drilling/production platforms, duty may be suspended under condition EU003 (certificate C990). This is a narrow exception.

Monitor CEPA ratification

Once the EU-Indonesia CEPA enters into force, duty rates may be reduced or eliminated. Exporters should monitor the agreement's ratification progress and prepare to issue origin declarations under the new agreement once it applies.
