How to Classify a Product Under 1515 90

Decision tree Worked examples Common pitfalls

A practical, step-by-step guide to determining the correct CN 8-digit code for any vegetable or microbial fat/oil falling under heading 1515 90. Follow the decision tree, check the worked examples, and avoid the common classification traps.

The Five-Gate Decision Tree

Classification under 1515 90 requires passing through five sequential decision points, or “gates.” Each gate narrows the code until only one CN 8-digit subheading remains.

1
Is it a fixed vegetable or microbial fat/oil, not chemically modified?
PASS if: The product is a naturally occurring (non-volatile, non-essential) fat or oil from a plant or microbial source, and it has not been boiled, oxidised, dehydrated, sulphurised, blown, polymerised, or otherwise chemically modified.

FAIL → If it is chemically modified, classify under heading 1518. If it is an essential oil or perfume material, classify under Chapter 33.
↓
2
Is it specifically named in another subheading of heading 1515?
Heading 1515 has named subheadings for: linseed oil (1515 11/19), maize/corn oil (1515 21/29), castor oil (1515 30), and sesame oil (1515 50). Also check other Chapter 15 headings: soya-bean (1507), groundnut (1508), olive (1509/1510), palm (1511), sunflower (1512), coconut/copra (1513), rape/colza (1514), raw caster oil, tung oil.

FAIL → If the product is specifically named, classify under that subheading. PASS → If it is not named, it falls under the catch-all 1515 90.
↓
3
Is it crude (unrefined) or refined/non-crude?
CRUDE: The oil has only been extracted (mechanically pressed or solvent extracted) and possibly filtered to remove solid impurities. No degumming, neutralisation, bleaching, or deodorisation. High FFA alone does not make an oil “refined” — crude oils can have high FFA naturally.

REFINED / NON-CRUDE: The oil has undergone any of: degumming, neutralisation (alkali refining), bleaching, deodorisation, winterisation, or any processing beyond simple extraction and filtration.
↓
4
Is it for technical or industrial use?
YES: The oil is destined for industrial processing — biodiesel feedstock, soap making, lubricant manufacture, paint/coating formulations, leather treatment, epoxy plasticisers, etc. The buyer is an industrial facility, not a food distributor or cosmetic brand. The oil is not fit for human consumption without further processing.

NO: The oil is destined for food, cosmetic, pharmaceutical, or direct consumer use.
↓
5
Is it solid, in packings of 1 kg or less?
This gate applies to solid fats only (e.g., shea butter, illipe butter, sal fat). Liquid/fluid oils always pass through to codes 59 or 99.

Solid ≤ 1 kg → code 51 (crude) or 91 (refined). These are consumer/retail packings and carry the highest duty (12.8%).

Solid > 1 kg, or fluid/liquid → code 59 (crude) or 99 (refined). These are bulk packings.

Example 5 — The Trap
Crude Neem Oil — is it technical or not?

Crude neem oil could be classified under 1515 90 40 (3.2%) if it is for biopesticide production (industrial use), or 1515 90 59 (6.4%) if it is for traditional medicine or cosmetic use. The duty difference is 3.2 percentage points.

The end use is determined by objective evidence: the purchase contract, the nature of the buyer (industrial pesticide manufacturer vs. herbal medicine distributor), and the product specifications. If the buyer is an agrochemical company, 1515 90 40 is defensible. If the buyer is a pharmacy or cosmetic brand, it is 1515 90 59.

Customs will challenge end-use claims
Declaring 1515 90 40 (3.2%) when the oil is actually for food/cosmetic use constitutes misdeclaration. Always obtain a BTI ruling for borderline cases.

Common Classification Pitfalls

Pitfall 1
“High FFA means it must be an acid oil (Chapter 38)”

Wrong. High free fatty acid content (even 25–35%) does not move a crude vegetable oil from Chapter 15 to Chapter 38. Heading 3823 (“acid oils from refining”) covers the acidulated by-product of alkali refining (soapstock that has been treated with acid), not a crude oil that naturally has high FFA. Classification follows the manufacturing process, not the chemical profile.

Pitfall 2
“If I refine it slightly (just filtered), it stays crude”

Correct — with a caveat. Mechanical filtering to remove seed meal, sediment, or solid impurities does not change crude status. However, if the oil has been degummed, neutralised (alkali-treated), bleached with bleaching earth, or deodorised with steam under vacuum, it is refined for classification purposes — even if the refining was minimal. The CN does not distinguish degrees of refining.

Pitfall 3
“Liquid oils in small bottles always stay at 6.4% or 9.6%”

Wrong. The ≤1 kg threshold in Gate 5 applies to both solid and liquid products. A 250 ml bottle of crude walnut oil weighs ~230 g — that is ≤ 1 kg, so it goes to 1515 90 51 (12.8%), not 1515 90 59 (6.4%). The 1 kg threshold is based on the net weight of the contents in the immediate packing.

Pitfall 4
“I can choose 1515 90 40 (3.2%) just by writing ‘industrial’ on the invoice”

Risky. Customs authorities look at objective evidence, not just a single word on an invoice. They will examine: who is the buyer? What do they manufacture? Is the oil fit for food without further processing? Is there a supply agreement? If the buyer is a food distributor, writing “industrial” on the invoice will not fool customs — and could trigger penalties for misdeclaration.

Pitfall 5
“Jojoba oil goes under 1515 90”

Technically yes, but check. Jojoba oil is explicitly mentioned in the heading 1515 heading text (“including jojoba oil”). It does not have its own subheading, so it falls under 1515 90. However, some jojoba “oil” on the market is actually a liquid wax ester, not a triglyceride oil. If it is classified as a wax rather than an oil, it may fall under heading 1521 (vegetable waxes) instead. Check the product's technical specification.

Evidence Checklist for Customs

Whatever code you declare, customs may ask for evidence. Prepare this documentation:

Gate Evidence needed Why customs asks
Gate 1 Certificate of analysis (botanical source, extraction method) Confirms it is a fixed vegetable oil, not an essential oil or chemically modified product
Gate 2 Product description and botanical name (Latin) Confirms the oil is not specifically named in another heading/subheading
Gate 3 Processing description; certificate of analysis (FFA, phosphorus, peroxide value) Distinguishes crude from refined. Low phosphorus + low FFA = likely refined. High FFA alone does not prove crude.
Gate 4 End-use declaration; supply agreement with EU buyer; purchase contract Proves the oil is genuinely for technical/industrial use (or not). This is the most scrutinised gate.
Gate 5 Packing list; net weight per immediate container Determines whether the ≤1 kg threshold applies
BTI: The gold standard
For any product imported regularly or in significant volume, apply for a Binding Tariff Information (BTI) ruling from the importing Member State's customs authority. A BTI is binding for 3 years across all 27 EU Member States and eliminates classification risk entirely.
