Frequently Asked Questions

Answers to the most common questions about importing vegetable oils into the EU, with a focus on rubber seed oil from Indonesia. Organised by topic for quick reference.

Classification

Q1
What HS code should I use for crude rubber seed oil?

Crude rubber seed oil for industrial use (e.g., biodiesel feedstock) should be classified under CN 1515 90 40 (crude fixed vegetable oils for technical/industrial use) at 3.2% MFN duty. If the oil is for food/cosmetic use, use 1515 90 59 at 6.4%. See Classification Guide.

Q2
My rubber seed oil has 30% FFA. Does that make it an “acid oil” under Chapter 38?

No. High FFA alone does not move a crude vegetable oil from Chapter 15 to Chapter 38. Heading 3823 (“acid oils from refining”) covers the acidulated by-product of alkali refining (soapstock treated with acid) — not a crude oil that naturally has high FFA. Classification follows the manufacturing process, not the chemical composition. See 3823 19 90 analysis.

Q3
Can I classify my oil under 3823 19 30 (PFAD) to get 2.9% instead of 3.2%?

Only if your product is genuinely a fatty acid distillate from physical refining (steam deodorisation). Rubber seed oil is a primary extracted oil, not a distillate — classifying it under 3823 19 30 would be misclassification. The 0.3pp savings is not worth the audit risk.

Q4
What is a BTI and do I need one?

A Binding Tariff Information (BTI) is a ruling from an EU Member State customs authority that legally confirms the HS/CN classification of a product. It is binding across all 27 EU Member States for up to 3 years. Strongly recommended for any product imported regularly or in volume, especially where the classification depends on end use (like 1515 90 40 vs. 59). Apply through the EU BTI system (EBTI).

Q5
Does filtering make an oil “refined”?

No. Mechanical filtering to remove seed meal, sediment, or solid impurities does not change crude status. However, degumming, neutralisation (alkali refining), bleaching, or deodorisation do make the oil “refined” for classification purposes — moving it from codes 40/51/59 to 60/91/99 with higher duty rates.

Duties & Rates

Q6
What duty does Indonesia pay on rubber seed oil?

Indonesia pays the full MFN rate because Section 3 (Animal and vegetable oils) was graduated from GSP on 1 January 2020. For crude RSO as industrial feedstock (1515 90 40): 3.2%. For crude RSO for food/cosmetic use (1515 90 59): 6.4%. See Indonesia page.

Q7
Does Indonesia get GSP preferences on heading 1515?

No. Indonesia's Section 3 (which includes all of Chapter 15, including heading 1515) was graduated from the EU GSP scheme effective 1 January 2020. Indonesia pays full MFN rates on all Chapter 15 products. Additionally, Indonesia will graduate entirely from GSP on 1 January 2027.

Q8
Will the EU-Indonesia CEPA reduce duties to zero?

Eventually, yes — but the CEPA is not yet in force (as of July 2026). Negotiations were concluded in September 2025, but the agreement must undergo legal revision, signature, and ratification by both parties. Once in force, tariffs will be progressively eliminated according to the agreement's phase-out schedule. Until then, full MFN rates apply.

Q9
Why does the same oil have different duty rates under different CN codes?

The EU uses tariff differentiation to protect domestic processors. Raw materials (crude oils for industrial use) face lower duties to support EU processing industries. Finished goods (refined oils, small-pack consumer products) face higher duties to protect EU consumer product manufacturers. This is standard tariff escalation.

Trade Defence (ADD / CVD)

Q10
Is there an anti-dumping duty on rubber seed oil from Indonesia?

No. The anti-dumping duty (Reg. 2023/111) applies only to industrial fatty acids under CN codes ex 3823 19 10 and ex 3823 19 90 — not to heading 1515. Rubber seed oil classified under 1515 90 40 or 1515 90 59 is not affected by any ADD or CVD.

Q11
What is “degree of split” (DoS) and why does it matter?

Degree of Split measures the percentage of fatty acid bonds that have been hydrolysed (split) from their glycerol backbone. DoS ≥97% means the product is almost pure free fatty acids. The EU's anti-dumping regulation (Reg. 2023/111) uses DoS ≥97% as its scope threshold: Indonesian fatty acids with DoS ≥97% face 46.4% ADD; those with DoS <97% are outside the scope.

Q12
If I certify my product for RED II biofuels, is the ADD waived?

Yes — for Chapter 38 fatty acids. Reg. 2023/111 explicitly excludes fatty acids certified for sustainable biofuels production under Directive (EU) 2018/2001 (RED II). You need ISCC EU or equivalent sustainability certification. However, this exemption only applies to the ADD on heading 3823 products — it is irrelevant to heading 1515 (which has no ADD anyway).

Blending & Mixtures

Q13
Can I mix RSO with another oil to get 0% duty?

Possibly — if you create a genuine mixture (at least 20% of each oil), the blend is inedible, and it's for industrial use, it could qualify for 1518 00 31 at 0% MFN. However, a token addition (e.g., 5% jatropha in 95% RSO) will be challenged by customs under GIR 2(b). See the Mixing Guide for full details.

Q14
Does the oil need to be physically blended before shipping?

Yes. Customs examines the product "as presented at import." If two oils arrive in separate tanks or containers but are invoiced as a blend, customs will classify each oil separately under its own heading. The blend must exist as a single, physically mixed product at the time of importation.

Q15
What is the CJEU Case C-233/15 and why does it matter?

This 2016 ruling established that for vegetable oil mixtures, the edible vs. inedible distinction is the determining factor for classification. An 88/12 rapeseed/sunflower blend was classified as inedible under 1518 00 31 based on the producer's own declarations about the production process — even though lab analysis didn't detect noxious substances. This means the producer's documentation and declared end-use carry significant weight.

Practical Importing

Q16
What documents do I need for each shipment?

Essential: (1) Commercial invoice with CN code, (2) Packing list, (3) Bill of lading, (4) Certificate of origin, (5) Certificate of analysis (FFA, moisture, IV, impurities), (6) End-use declaration (especially for 1515 90 40). For blends: (7) Blend composition certificate. For organic: (8) COI (Certificate of Inspection, Reg. 2021/2306).

Q17
Can I import in small volumes (e.g., 1–5 tonnes for testing)?

Yes. There is no minimum volume for customs declaration. However, for small trial shipments, consider using the Simplified Declaration Procedure or Entry in the Declarant's Records (Article 182 UCC), which reduces administrative burden. The duty rate is the same regardless of volume. For trial biodiesel feedstock testing, most EU plants can accept samples of 200–1,000 litres in IBC totes.

Q18
How long does a BTI application take?

Typically 4–6 months from submission to decision. The customs authority may request samples, technical documentation, and processing descriptions. Once issued, the BTI is valid for 3 years and binding across all EU Member States. Apply well before your first commercial shipment.

Q19
What happens if customs reclassifies my product after import?

Post-clearance audits can occur for up to 3 years after import. If customs reclassifies the product to a higher-duty code, you face: (1) back-duties for the difference, (2) interest on the unpaid amount, and potentially (3) penalties for misdeclaration. This is why BTI and proper documentation are critical — especially for end-use-dependent codes like 1515 90 40 vs. 59.

Q20
Do I need ISCC EU certification for biodiesel feedstock?

Not for customs purposes — ISCC EU certification is a sustainability requirement under RED II, not a customs requirement. However, if the EU biodiesel producer wants to count the fuel toward renewable energy targets, the feedstock must be sustainability-certified. ISCC EU (International Sustainability and Carbon Certification) is the most widely used scheme. The ADD biofuel exemption (Reg. 2023/111, for Chapter 38 products) also requires RED II certification.

Q21
Which EU Member State is best to import through?

The duty rate is the same in all 27 Member States (it's the Common Customs Tariff). The main differences are: Netherlands (Rotterdam — largest bulk liquid port, experienced with vegetable oils), Spain (Barcelona, Bilbao — strong biodiesel industry), Germany (Hamburg — efficient customs, strong chemical industry), Italy (Trieste, Genoa — Mediterranean gateway). For BTI, choose the Member State where you'll most frequently import.

Terminology & Concepts

Q22
What is the difference between MFN, ERGA OMNES, and GSP rates?

MFN (Most-Favoured-Nation) and ERGA OMNES are the same thing — the standard duty rate applied to all WTO members without preferential agreements. GSP (Generalised Scheme of Preferences) is a reduced rate for eligible developing countries. FTA rates (e.g., EU-Japan EPA) are preferential rates under bilateral agreements, often 0%. The effective rate depends on the origin country and available preferences.

Q23
What is “Section 3 graduation”?

Under GSP Article 8, when a beneficiary country's share of EU GSP imports in a product section exceeds 15% (12.5% for textiles) for 3 consecutive years, preferences for that entire section are suspended. Indonesia's Section 3 (animal and vegetable oils, Chapter 15) was graduated on 1 January 2020. This means all Chapter 15 products from Indonesia pay full MFN rates.

Q24
What does “ex” mean before a CN code (e.g., ex 3823 19 90)?

The “ex” prefix means “extracted from” — it indicates that only certain products within that CN code are covered, not all products under the code. For example, ex 3823 19 90 in the anti-dumping regulation means only those TARIC subcodes of 3823 19 90 that meet the specific criteria (DoS ≥97%) are within the ADD scope — not all products classified under 3823 19 90.

Q25
What is the difference between ADD and CVD?

Anti-Dumping Duty (ADD) counters dumping — when products are sold in the EU below their normal value (i.e., below the domestic price in the exporting country). Countervailing Duty (CVD) counters subsidies — when the exporting country's government provides financial subsidies that give the product an unfair advantage. Both are trade defence instruments under WTO rules. For Indonesian fatty products: ADD applies to Chapter 38 fatty acids (Reg. 2023/111); CVD applies to biodiesel (Reg. 2019/2092).
