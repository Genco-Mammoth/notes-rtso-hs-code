Crude rubber seed oil for industrial use (e.g., biodiesel feedstock) should be classified under CN 1515 90 40 (crude fixed vegetable oils for technical or industrial use) at 3.2% MFN duty.

If the oil is intended for food or cosmetic use, the correct code is 1515 90 59 (6.4% MFN). The critical distinction is declared end-use, not chemical composition. See the Classification Guide for the full decision tree.

A Binding Tariff Information (BTI) is a legally binding classification ruling issued by an EU Member State customs authority. It confirms the correct CN code for your specific product and is binding across all 27 Member States for up to 3 years.

Strongly recommended for any product imported regularly or in volume, especially where the classification depends on end-use (like 1515 90 40 vs. 1515 90 59). Having a BTI eliminates classification risk and protects against post-clearance reclassification. Apply through the EU BTI system (EBTI).

No. Mechanical filtering to remove seed meal, sediment, or solid impurities does not change crude status. Winterisation (chilling to remove waxes) is also considered a physical process that preserves crude classification.

However, degumming, neutralisation (alkali refining), bleaching, or deodorisation do make the oil “refined” for CN purposes — moving it from codes 40/51/59 (crude) to 60/91/99 (refined) with higher duty rates.

The end-use declaration is the critical document for claiming 1515 90 40 (3.2%) instead of 1515 90 59 (6.4%). You need:

  • A written end-use declaration stating the oil is exclusively for technical/industrial purposes (not human or animal consumption, not cosmetic)
  • Supporting evidence: contract with biodiesel producer, refinery processing agreement, or internal production records showing the oil enters an industrial process
  • The declaration should specify the specific industrial use (e.g., “feedstock for biodiesel production via transesterification”)

Customs may require the importer to maintain records demonstrating the oil was actually used as declared. Some Member States require an end-use authorisation in advance under the TARIC end-use procedure.

Classification follows the General Interpretative Rules (GIR) in hierarchical order:

  • GIR 1: The heading text and Section/Chapter Notes determine classification first — never jump to GIR 2–6 unless GIR 1 is unclear
  • GIR 3(a): The most specific description prevails (e.g., “rubber seed oil” under 1515 is more specific than “vegetable oil mixture” under 1518)
  • GIR 3(b): If still ambiguous, classify by the component that gives the product its essential character
  • GIR 3(c): If neither (a) nor (b) resolves it, use the heading that appears last in numerical order

In practice, for rubber seed oil, GIR 1 clearly places it in heading 1515 (other fixed vegetable fats and oils), not 1518 or 3823, unless it has undergone specific processing that changes its character.

The EU uses tariff escalation to protect domestic processing industries:

  • Raw materials (crude oils for industrial use, e.g., 1515 90 40 at 3.2%) face lower duties to support EU processors who add value through refining or biodiesel production
  • Semi-processed goods (crude oils for food, 1515 90 59 at 6.4%) face moderate duties
  • Finished goods (refined oils, 1515 90 99 at 9.6%; consumer-packaged products often higher) face higher duties to protect EU consumer product manufacturers

This is standard WTO-consistent tariff policy — the more processing a product has undergone, the higher the duty, encouraging processing to occur within the EU.

MFN (Most-Favoured-Nation) and ERGA OMNES are the same thing — the standard WTO duty rate applied to all WTO members without preferential agreements. “Erga omnes” is Latin for “towards all” and is the term used in TARIC.

GSP (Generalised Scheme of Preferences) is a reduced rate for eligible developing countries. Under the EU's Standard GSP, duties are reduced or eliminated for approximately 66% of tariff lines. GSP+ (for vulnerable countries) and EBA (Everything But Arms, for LDCs) offer deeper preferences.

FTA rates (e.g., under the future EU-Indonesia CEPA) are preferential rates under bilateral agreements, often progressively eliminated to 0%. The effective rate depends on origin country, product, and available preferences.

From 1 January 2027, Indonesia graduates entirely from GSP (Reg. (EU) 2025/1951). This means all Indonesian products — including those in sections that retained GSP preferences — will pay full MFN rates.

For rubber seed oil, the practical impact is zero: Section 3 was already graduated in 2020, so Indonesia already pays MFN on heading 1515. The 2027 graduation primarily affects products in other sections (e.g., chemicals, machinery, textiles).

The real game-changer will be the EU-Indonesia CEPA, which will progressively reduce MFN rates to zero — but only after it enters into force (likely 2028+).

No. The anti-dumping duty (Reg. (EU) 2023/111) applies only to industrial fatty acids under CN codes ex 3823 19 10 and ex 3823 19 90 with a Degree of Split (DoS) ≥97%. It does not apply to heading 1515 products.

Rubber seed oil classified under 1515 90 40 or 1515 90 59 is not affected by any ADD or CVD from Indonesia. This is a major advantage of the Chapter 15 classification path over Chapter 38.

Degree of Split (DoS) measures the percentage of fatty acid bonds that have been hydrolysed (split) from their glycerol backbone. A DoS of 97% means 97% of the triglyceride molecules have been broken into free fatty acids — the product is almost entirely free fatty acids.

The EU's anti-dumping regulation (Reg. (EU) 2023/111) uses DoS ≥97% as its product scope threshold:

  • Indonesian fatty acids with DoS ≥97% under 3823 19 10 or 3823 19 90: subject to 46.4% ADD
  • Indonesian fatty acids with DoS <97%: outside the ADD scope
  • Products under heading 1515 (triglyceride oils): DoS is irrelevant as there is no ADD

However, this exemption is only relevant for products classified in heading 3823. For products classified under heading 1515 (including rubber seed oil), no ADD applies regardless, so the RED II exemption is unnecessary. This is another reason the 1515 path is preferred.

Feedstock imports face only the MFN customs duty plus any applicable ADD (which, as noted, does not apply to heading 1515).

Yes. Customs examines the product “as presented at import.” If two oils arrive in separate tanks, containers, or compartments but are invoiced together as a “blend,” customs will classify each oil separately under its own heading.

The blend must exist as a single, physically mixed product at the time of importation. Co-loading in a single tank is acceptable provided the oils are genuinely mixed. Blending at origin is strongly recommended over shipboard blending.

This 2016 ruling (SIA “Baltkomerc” vs. Valsts ieņēmumu dienests) established that for vegetable oil mixtures, the edible vs. inedible distinction is the determining factor for classification under CN code 1518.

Key findings:

  • An 88/12 rapeseed/sunflower blend was classified as inedible under 1518 00 31 based on the producer's own declarations about the production process
  • This was upheld even though laboratory analysis did not detect noxious substances
  • The producer's documentation and declared end-use carry significant weight in classification — chemical testing alone does not determine edibility

This ruling is directly relevant for RSO blends: if the producer documents the blend as inedible and for industrial use, this strongly supports classification under 1518 00 31.

The same principles apply whether a blend has 2, 3, or more components:

  • GIR 3(b) applies: classify by the component giving the mixture its essential character
  • If no single component dominates, and the mixture is inedible and for industrial use, 1518 00 31 at 0% may apply
  • All components must be different types of vegetable oil — mixing the same oil from different origins does not create a “mixture” for CN purposes
  • Include all components in the blend composition certificate with exact percentages

A 3-way blend (e.g., 40% RSO + 35% jatropha + 25% Pongamia) that is inedible and for biodiesel is a strong candidate for 1518 00 31. See Mixing Guide for worked examples.

Essential documents for every shipment:

  1. Commercial invoice — with declared CN code and value
  2. Packing list — container/tank details, weights, volumes
  3. Bill of lading — transport document
  4. Certificate of origin — issued by Indonesian authorities (Form A if GSP-eligible, though Section 3 is graduated)
  5. Certificate of analysis — FFA%, moisture, iodine value, impurities, phosphorus
  6. End-use declaration — mandatory for 1515 90 40 (industrial use)

Additional for blends: blend composition certificate with exact percentages. For organic claims: COI (Certificate of Inspection, Reg. (EU) 2021/2306).

For trial shipments:

  • Consider the Simplified Declaration Procedure or Entry in the Declarant's Records (Article 182 UCC) to reduce administrative burden
  • Most EU biodiesel plants can accept samples of 200–1,000 litres in IBC totes for feedstock testing
  • A BTI is still recommended before commercial volumes, but not strictly required for genuine trials
  • For very small quantities (under 5 tonnes), some Member States accept a simplified customs procedure with reduced documentation

Typically 4–6 months from submission to decision, though complex cases can take longer. The customs authority may request:

  • Product samples for laboratory analysis
  • Detailed technical documentation (extraction method, processing steps, chemical profile)
  • Manufacturing process descriptions and flowcharts
  • End-use declarations and supporting contracts

Once issued, the BTI is valid for 3 years and binding across all 27 EU Member States. Apply well before your first commercial shipment. You can check existing BTI decisions for similar products through the EBTI database.

Post-clearance audits can occur for up to 3 years after import (5 years in some Member States for serious negligence). If customs reclassifies the product to a higher-duty code, you face:

  1. Back-duties — the difference between the duty paid and the correct higher rate, for all shipments in the audit period
  2. Interest on the unpaid amount (rates vary by Member State, typically 3–8% per annum)
  3. Penalties for misdeclaration — can range from administrative fines to criminal penalties for deliberate misclassification

This is why a BTI and proper documentation are critical — a valid BTI provides a complete defence against reclassification during its validity period. End-use-dependent codes like 1515 90 40 are particularly scrutinised.

The duty rate is identical in all 27 Member States (Common Customs Tariff). Differences are operational:

  • Netherlands (Rotterdam) — largest bulk liquid port in Europe, extensive tank storage, experienced customs brokers for veg oils
  • Spain (Barcelona, Bilbao) — strong domestic biodiesel industry, shorter transit from Indonesia via Suez
  • Germany (Hamburg) — efficient customs administration, strong chemical industry for offtake
  • Italy (Trieste, Genoa) — Mediterranean gateway, closer to Asian shipping routes
  • Belgium (Antwerp) — major chemical cluster, extensive tank storage

For BTI applications, choose the Member State where you'll most frequently import. The BTI is valid across all Member States regardless of which one issued it.

Customs value is determined under the Union Customs Code (UCC), primarily using the transaction value method (Article 70 UCC): the price actually paid or payable for the goods, adjusted for:

  • Additions: commissions, packing costs, royalties, transport/insurance to the EU border, assists (materials/tools supplied by buyer)
  • Deductions: post-import transport within the EU, EU construction/assembly costs, EU duties and taxes

For related-party transactions (e.g., Indonesian supplier and EU importer under common ownership), customs will scrutinise whether the transfer price reflects arm's length pricing. If not, alternative valuation methods apply. Maintain transfer pricing documentation demonstrating the price is consistent with market benchmarks for comparable crude oils.

Yes. Reg. (EU) 2023/1115 (EUDR) applies to rubber as a commodity, and products derived from rubber trees — including rubber seed oil — fall within its scope. The EUDR requires that products placed on the EU market be:

  • Deforestation-free — produced on land not subject to deforestation after 31 December 2020
  • Produced legally — in compliance with all relevant laws of the country of production
  • Covered by a due diligence statement — submitted through the EU's Information System before import

The regulation applies from 30 December 2025 for large operators (30 December 2026 for SMEs). Rubber seed oil importers must conduct due diligence on the plantation origin of the seeds. However, rubber seed oil as a by-product of existing rubber latex production (rather than a driver of new plantations) may simplify compliance.

The due diligence statement (Article 4 of EUDR) must include:

  • Product description — CN code, quantity, species (Hevea brasiliensis)
  • Country of production — Indonesia, and specific region/plantation if available
  • Geolocation — geographic coordinates of all plots of land where the rubber seeds were harvested
  • Time period — date or time range of production
  • Deforestation risk assessment — documentation of the risk analysis performed
  • Risk mitigation measures — if any risk was identified
  • Operator/trader details — EORI number, contact information

For rubber seed oil collected from existing rubber plantations (which produce latex as the primary product), the deforestation risk is generally low, but documentation of plantation establishment dates (pre-2020) is essential.

Under Article 29 of the EUDR, the European Commission will classify countries (or parts thereof) into three risk categories:

  • Low risk — simplified due diligence (only document collection, no risk assessment/mitigation required)
  • Standard risk — full due diligence required
  • High risk — full due diligence plus enhanced scrutiny by competent authorities (9% of operators subject to checks vs. 3% for standard risk)

The benchmarking list was initially due by December 2024 but has been delayed. Indonesia's classification is not yet final but is expected to be standard or high risk given deforestation trends. Importers should prepare for full due diligence requirements regardless.

The “ex” prefix means “extracted from” — it indicates that only certain products within that CN code are covered by the measure, not all products under the code.

For example, ex 3823 19 90 in the ADD regulation means only those TARIC subcodes of 3823 19 90 that meet the specific criteria (DoS ≥97%, Indonesian origin, not RED II certified) are within the ADD scope. Other products classified under 3823 19 90 — such as acid oils with DoS <97% — are not subject to the ADD even though they share the same 8-digit CN code.

In TARIC, “ex” codes are implemented through 10-digit TARIC subcodes and additional TARIC codes that precisely define which products are covered.

Anti-Dumping Duty (ADD) counters dumping — when products are sold in the EU below their normal value (domestic price in the exporting country). The ADD bridges the gap between the export price and the normal value (the “dumping margin”). For Indonesian fatty acids: 46.4% (Wilmar), up to 60.6% for others.

Countervailing Duty (CVD) counters subsidies — when the exporting country's government provides financial contributions that give the product an unfair advantage. The CVD offsets the subsidy amount. For Indonesian biodiesel: 8%–18% depending on exporter.

Both are WTO-consistent trade defence instruments but target different unfair trade practices. They can be applied simultaneously to the same product (as with Indonesian biodiesel). Neither applies to rubber seed oil under heading 1515.

Yes. There is a two-stage appeal process:

  1. Administrative review — submit a request for review to the customs authority that made the decision, typically within 30–60 days (varies by Member State). Include supporting evidence: laboratory analysis, expert opinions, BTI rulings for similar products, and technical documentation.
  2. Judicial appeal — if the administrative review is unsuccessful, appeal to the national tax/customs court (or equivalent tribunal) in the relevant Member State. Further appeal to the national supreme court and ultimately a preliminary reference to the CJEU (Court of Justice of the European Union) on questions of EU law.

Having a BTI for the same product effectively pre-empts classification disputes. If a Member State customs authority issues a BTI for a specific CN code, other Member States must respect it.

While CN codes are 8-digit and uniform across the EU, TARIC codes add 2 more digits (10-digit total) to capture EU-specific measures that don't exist in the common CN:

  • Tariff suspensions and quotas — temporary duty reductions for specific products
  • Anti-dumping and countervailing duties — TARIC subcodes identify which products are in-scope (e.g., 3823 19 90 10 for ADD-applicable acid oils vs. 3823 19 90 90 for others)
  • Import/export restrictions — prohibitions, licences, surveillance
  • End-use procedures — products eligible for reduced duties if used for specific purposes

In practice, traders declare at the 10-digit TARIC level, not just the 8-digit CN. The TARIC code determines the actual duty payable. Always check the TARIC database (TARIC Consultation) for your specific product and origin, not just the CN nomenclature.
