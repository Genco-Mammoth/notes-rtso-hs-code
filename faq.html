Frequently Asked Questions

Answers to the most common questions about importing vegetable oils into the EU, with a focus on rubber seed oil from Indonesia. Organised by topic for quick reference.

Classification

Q1
What HS code should I use for crude rubber seed oil?

Crude rubber seed oil for industrial use (e.g., biodiesel feedstock) should be classified under CN 1515 90 40 (crude fixed vegetable oils for technical/industrial use) at 3.2% MFN duty. If the oil is for food/cosmetic use, use 1515 90 59 at 6.4%. See Classification Guide.

Q2
My rubber seed oil has 30% FFA. Does that make it an “acid oil” under Chapter 38?

No. High FFA alone does not move a crude oil from Chapter 15 to Chapter 38. Heading 3823 covers the acidulated by-product of alkali refining (soapstock treated with acid) — not a crude oil that naturally has high FFA. Classification follows the manufacturing process, not chemical composition. See 3823 19 90 analysis.

Q3
Can I classify my oil under 3823 19 30 (PFAD) to get 2.9% instead of 3.2%?

Only if your product is genuinely a fatty acid distillate from physical refining. Rubber seed oil is a primary extracted oil, not a distillate — classifying it under 3823 19 30 would be misclassification. The 0.3pp savings is not worth the audit risk. See 3823 19 30 analysis.

Q4
What is a BTI and do I need one?

A Binding Tariff Information (BTI) is a legally binding classification ruling from an EU customs authority. It confirms the correct CN code and is binding across all 27 Member States for up to 3 years. Strongly recommended if classification depends on end-use (1515 90 40 vs. 59). Apply through the EU BTI system (EBTI).

Q5
Does filtering make an oil “refined”?

No. Mechanical filtering and winterisation do not change crude status. However, degumming, neutralisation, bleaching, or deodorisation make the oil refined — moving it from codes 40/51/59 to 60/91/99 with higher duty rates.

Q6
How do I document “industrial use” for 1515 90 40?

You need a written end-use declaration stating the oil is exclusively for technical/industrial purposes, plus supporting evidence (contract with biodiesel producer). Some Member States require advance end-use authorisation under TARIC.

Q7
If my product fits multiple CN codes, which takes priority?

Classification follows GIR 1 (heading text/notes), then GIR 3(a) (most specific description), then GIR 3(b) (essential character), then GIR 3(c) (last numerical heading). For RSO, GIR 1 clearly places it in heading 1515.

Duties & Rates

Q8
What duty does Indonesia pay on rubber seed oil?

Indonesia pays the full MFN rate because Section 3 was graduated from GSP on 1 January 2020. For crude RSO as industrial feedstock (1515 90 40): 3.2%. For food/cosmetic use (1515 90 59): 6.4%. See Indonesia page.

Q9
Does Indonesia get GSP preferences on heading 1515?

No. Indonesia's Section 3 was graduated from GSP effective 1 January 2020. Indonesia will graduate entirely from GSP on 1 January 2027 (Reg. (EU) 2025/1951).

Q10
Will the EU-Indonesia CEPA reduce duties to zero?

Eventually, yes — but CEPA is not yet in force (as of July 2026). Negotiations concluded September 2025; ratification takes time. Realistic timeline: late 2027 at the earliest for provisional application. Until then, full MFN rates apply.

Q11
Why does the same oil have different duty rates?

The EU uses tariff escalation: raw materials face lower duties (3.2%) to support EU processors; finished goods face higher duties (9.6%) to protect EU manufacturers. The more processing, the higher the rate.

Q12
What is the difference between MFN, ERGA OMNES, and GSP?

MFN and ERGA OMNES are the same — the standard WTO rate. GSP is a reduced rate for developing countries. GSP+ and EBA offer deeper preferences. FTA rates (e.g., future EU-Indonesia CEPA) are bilateral preferential rates.

Q13
What happens when Indonesia graduates from GSP in 2027?

From 1 January 2027, all Indonesian products pay full MFN. For RSO, impact is zero — Section 3 was already graduated in 2020. The real change will be the EU-Indonesia CEPA once it enters into force.

Trade Defence (ADD / CVD)

Q14
Is there an anti-dumping duty on rubber seed oil from Indonesia?

No. The ADD (Reg. 2023/111) applies only to industrial fatty acids under ex 3823 19 10 and ex 3823 19 90 — not heading 1515. RSO is not affected by any ADD or CVD.

Q15
What is “Degree of Split” (DoS) and why does it matter?

DoS measures the percentage of fatty acids hydrolysed from glycerol. DoS ≥97% means nearly pure FFA. The EU's ADD uses DoS ≥97% as its threshold: Indonesian fatty acids meeting this face 46.4% ADD; those below are outside scope.

Q16
If I certify for RED II biofuels, is the ADD waived?

Yes — for Chapter 38 fatty acids only. Reg. 2023/111 excludes fatty acids certified for sustainable biofuels under RED II (ISCC EU). For heading 1515 products, no ADD applies regardless.

Q17
Does the CVD on Indonesian biodiesel affect feedstock?

No. The CVD (Reg. 2019/2092, 8–18%) applies to finished biodiesel (FAME), not feedstock. RSO is a feedstock and outside the CVD scope.

Blending & Mixtures

Q18
Can I mix RSO with another oil to get 0% duty?

Possibly — a genuine inedible blend (at least 20% each) for industrial use could qualify for 1518 00 31 at 0%. Token additions (e.g., 5% jatropha) will be challenged. See Mixing Guide.

Q19
Must the blend be physically created before shipping?

Yes. Customs examines the product “as presented at import.” Separate tanks = separate products. The blend must be a single, physically mixed product at import.

Q20
What is CJEU Case C-233/15 and why does it matter?

This 2016 ruling established that the edible/inedible distinction is the key factor for classifying oil mixtures. An 88/12 rapeseed/sunflower blend was classified as inedible under 1518 00 31 based on the producer's declarations — even without lab evidence of noxious substances.

Q21
How are mixtures with 3+ oils classified?

Same principles as 2-way blends. GIR 3(b) applies: classify by the component giving essential character. If none dominates and the blend is inedible/industrial, 1518 00 31 at 0% may apply.

Practical Importing

Q22
What documents do I need for each shipment?

Essential: (1) Commercial invoice with CN code, (2) Packing list, (3) Bill of lading, (4) Certificate of origin, (5) Certificate of analysis (FFA, moisture, IV, impurities), (6) End-use declaration (for 1515 90 40). For blends: (7) Blend composition certificate.

Q23
Can I import small volumes (1–5 tonnes) for testing?

Yes. No minimum volume for customs. Consider Simplified Declaration Procedure or Entry in Declarant's Records (Article 182 UCC). Most EU biodiesel plants accept 200–1,000 litre samples in IBC totes.

Q24
How long does a BTI application take?

Typically 4–6 months from submission to decision. Customs may request samples, technical documentation, and processing descriptions. Valid for 3 years, binding across all 27 Member States.

Q25
What happens if customs reclassifies my product after import?

Post-clearance audits can occur up to 3 years after import. If reclassified upward, you face back-duties, interest, and potential penalties. A BTI provides complete defence during its validity.

Q26
Do I need ISCC EU certification for biodiesel feedstock?

Not for customs — ISCC EU is a RED II sustainability requirement, not a customs requirement. However, EU biodiesel buyers typically require it to count fuel toward renewable targets.

Q27
Which EU Member State is best to import through?

Duty rates are identical in all 27 Member States. Operational differences: Netherlands (Rotterdam — largest bulk port), Spain (strong biodiesel industry), Germany (efficient customs), Italy (closer to Asian shipping). For BTI, choose your main import Member State.

Q28
How does customs valuation work for vegetable oil imports?

Under the Union Customs Code, valuation uses the transaction value method (price paid + adjustments). For related-party transactions, customs scrutinises whether transfer pricing reflects arm's length pricing.

EUDR & Sustainability

Q29
Does the EU Deforestation Regulation (EUDR) apply to rubber seed oil?

Yes. Reg. (EU) 2023/1115 applies to rubber and its derivatives. The oil must be deforestation-free, legally produced, and covered by a due diligence statement. RSO as a by-product of existing rubber plantations may simplify compliance.

Q30
What does the EUDR due diligence statement require?

Product description & CN code, country/region of production, geolocation of harvest plots, time period, deforestation risk assessment, risk mitigation measures, and operator details (EORI number).

Q31
How does EUDR country benchmarking affect Indonesia?

The Commission will classify countries as low/standard/high risk. Indonesia is expected to be standard or high risk. Importers should prepare for full due diligence requirements regardless.

Advanced

Q32
What does “ex” mean before a CN code (e.g., ex 3823 19 90)?

“ex” means only certain products within that CN code are covered by the measure. For example, ex 3823 19 90 in the ADD regulation covers only products meeting specific criteria (DoS ≥97%). “ex” codes are implemented through 10-digit TARIC subcodes.

Q33
What is the difference between ADD and CVD?

ADD (anti-dumping) counters dumping — selling below normal value. CVD (countervailing duty) counters subsidies. For Indonesia: ADD applies to Chapter 38 fatty acids (46.4%); CVD applies to biodiesel (8–18%). Neither applies to RSO under heading 1515.

Q34
Can I appeal a customs classification decision?

Yes. Two-stage process: (1) Administrative review with the customs authority, then (2) Judicial appeal to the national customs court and ultimately the CJEU. A BTI effectively pre-empts classification disputes.

Q35
How are TARIC codes different from CN codes in practice?

CN codes are 8-digit; TARIC codes add 2 digits (10-digit) for EU-specific measures: tariff suspensions, ADD/CVD subcodes, import restrictions, end-use procedures. Traders declare at the 10-digit TARIC level. Always check the TARIC database.
