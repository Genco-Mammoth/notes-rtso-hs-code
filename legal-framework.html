1. The Foundational Law: Regulation (EEC) No 2658/87

Council Regulation (EEC) No 2658/87 of 23 July 1987 — “on the tariff and statistical nomenclature and on the Common Customs Tariff” — is the single most important legal instrument in EU customs tariff law. It established both the Combined Nomenclature (CN) and the Integrated Tariff (TARIC), and remains the parent regulation to this day, over 38 years later.

Key provisions of Regulation 2658/87
ArticleWhat it does
Art. 1 Establishes the Combined Nomenclature, comprising:
(a) the Harmonized System nomenclature (6-digit, managed by the WCO);
(b) EU subdivisions called CN subheadings (digits 7–8);
(c) preliminary provisions, section/chapter notes, and footnotes.
Art. 2 Mandates the Commission to establish TARIC — the Integrated Tariff of the European Union — with additional subdivisions (digits 9–10) for goods subject to specific EU measures.
Art. 4 Defines the Common Customs Tariff (CCT) as the combination of the CN, the duty rates, and all tariff measures in TARIC.
Art. 6 Gives the European Commission (DG TAXUD) responsibility for management and daily publication of TARIC.
Art. 7–10 Establishes the Nomenclature Committee (also called the “TARIC Committee”) and sets out the comitology procedure for adopting nomenclature measures.
Art. 12 Mandates the annual CN update: a complete version of Annex I (with duty rates) must be published in the Official Journal by 31 October each year, applying from 1 January of the following year.

Reg. 2658/87 — Consolidated text (EUR-Lex)

What is the Common Customs Tariff (CCT)?

The CCT is not a single codified statute but rather a composite legal concept. As the European Commission states:

Official Definition
“The tariff is the name given to the combination of the nomenclature and the duty rates which apply to each classification.” European Commission, DG TAXUD

The CCT applies uniformly across all 27 EU Member States. When goods enter the EU from any non-EU country, the same classification rules and duty rates apply regardless of which Member State is the point of entry.

2. The Annual Update Cycle

The Combined Nomenclature is not static. Under Article 12 of Regulation 2658/87, the Commission adopts a new complete version of Annex I every year via an Implementing Regulation. This annual regulation is the legal instrument that sets the duty rates for the coming year.

Key distinction
The annual CN Implementing Regulations define the classification structure (which codes exist, how products are described). The duty rates themselves are set separately in Council Regulations (see below). For HS 1515.90, the current rates originate from Council Regulation (EU) 2024/1652.

3. Authoritative Bodies

Three institutional actors play distinct roles in setting and applying EU customs duties:

Council of the EU

The legislative authority that adopts foundational regulations and major duty changes. It adopted Regulation 2658/87, Regulation 978/2012 (GSP), and Regulation 2024/1652 (duty increases). Under Art. 10(3), the Council can override Commission nomenclature measures by qualified majority within three months.

Role: Sets the law

European Commission — DG TAXUD

The Directorate-General for Taxation and Customs Union is the operational engine. It manages and publishes TARIC daily, drafts the annual CN update, issues classification regulations and Binding Tariff Information (BTI) decisions, and transmits TARIC data to national customs authorities.

Role: Manages & publishes

Nomenclature Committee

Also known as the “TARIC Committee”, established by Art. 7. Composed of Member State representatives, chaired by the Commission. Examines all matters concerning classification, explanatory notes, and amendments. Operates via comitology (Art. 10): votes on draft measures; disagreements are referred to the Council.

Role: Reviews & approves

Supporting bodies

World Customs Organization (WCO)
Manages the 6-digit Harmonized System (HS) internationally. The EU’s CN is built on top of the HS. HS revisions occur roughly every 5 years (HS 2012, 2017, 2022, 2027).
Court of Justice of the European Union (CJEU)
Issues binding rulings on tariff classification disputes. CJEU judgments can change how codes are interpreted and trigger CN amendments.
National Customs Authorities
Each Member State operates its own customs administration. They apply the CCT using TARIC data transmitted daily by DG TAXUD. They may also use additional national statistical codes beyond the 10-digit TARIC level.

4. GSP: Regulation (EU) No 978/2012

The EU’s Generalised Scheme of Preferences (GSP) is the legal framework under which developing countries receive reduced or zero-duty access to the EU market. It is established by Regulation (EU) No 978/2012 of the European Parliament and Council, adopted 25 October 2012, applicable since 1 January 2014.

Standard GSP

Partial/full duty removal on ~66% of tariff lines. Non-sensitive products: 0% duty. Sensitive products: 3.5 percentage-point reduction on the MFN rate.

For low and lower-middle income countries. Automatic eligibility.

GSP+

Zero duty on the same ~66% of tariff lines, including sensitive products.

For vulnerable countries that ratify and implement 27+ international conventions on human rights, labour, environment, good governance.

EBA (Everything But Arms)

Duty-free, quota-free access for all products except arms/ammunition (Ch. 93).

For UN-classified Least Developed Countries (LDCs). Automatic. The most generous arrangement.

GSP Graduation by Section (Article 8)

Under Article 8, tariff preferences for a specific product section are suspended (“graduated”) when imports from a beneficiary country exceed a threshold relative to all GSP imports. This is an automatic mechanism — not a political decision.

How the mechanism works

Step What happens Legal basis
1 Eurostat monitors EU imports from each GSP beneficiary by section, annually Art. 8(4)
2 Threshold check: did the country's 3-year average share exceed 15% (or 12.5% for textiles)? Art. 8(1), Annex VI
3 Commission adopts implementing act listing graduated sections (advisory procedure) Art. 8(1)
4 Preferences suspended from 1 January of the following year. All GSP preferences for that section cease. Art. 8(1)
5 Triennial review: every 3 years, the list is revisited. If the share drops below threshold, preferences can be restored. Art. 8(3)
Relevance to this report
Indonesia’s exports in Section 3 (Animal and vegetable oils) — which includes heading 1515 — were graduated from Standard GSP preferences on 1 January 2020. This means Indonesia pays the full MFN rate on these codes. See the Indonesia page for full details.

5. Russia/Belarus Sanctions: Regulation (EU) 2024/1652

Council Regulation (EU) 2024/1652 of 24 June 2024 is the regulation that currently sets the duty rates for HS 1515.90 codes. Despite its short title — “amending Annex I to Regulation (EEC) No 2658/87 as regards certain products originating in or exported from the Russian Federation and the Republic of Belarus” — this regulation had a broader impact than its title suggests.

What the regulation actually did

The regulation’s stated purpose was to prevent market disruption from Russian and Belarusian agricultural imports by increasing duties on those origins to 50%. However, to implement this, the regulation restated the duty rates for the affected CN codes in Annex I, creating a two-tier structure via footnote (6):

Footnote (6) — The key provision
“Customs duty applicable, except for products originating in or exported directly or indirectly from the Russian Federation and the Republic of Belarus, where an ad valorem duty rate of 50% applies.”

⚠ Source Discrepancy on Pre-Existing Rates

There is a discrepancy across databases about whether the rates stated in Reg. 2024/1652 (e.g., 6.4% for 1515 90 59) were newly introduced by this regulation (meaning they were previously 0%/duty-free) or whether they pre-existed and were merely restated in the regulation to attach the RU/BY footnote.

EUR-Lex (primary) The regulation text states rates with footnote (6) without indicating whether they are new or pre-existing. Recitals note that “those tariffs are either set at zero or very low” for some products.
tariffnumber.com 2025 and 2026 editions show the rates as 6.4% and 9.6%, citing Reg. 2024/1652 as legal basis.
celna24.pl Shows ERGA OMNES (excl. RU/BY) as 0% for these codes, with only RU/BY at 50%.
Verdict: The EUR-Lex primary text is authoritative. The rates explicitly stated in the regulation Annex (6,4% and 9,6%) are the legally applicable conventional duty rates for all origins except RU/BY. Third-party databases showing 0% for non-RU/BY origins are not consistent with the regulation text. See the Sources page for full analysis.

How to read this
Each layer below builds on the one above. A single import of 1515 90 59 crude oil from Indonesia touches all six layers: the WCO defines what the product is internationally, Regulation 2658/87 provides the legal framework, the annual CN regulation defines the exact 8-digit code, Regulation 2024/1652 sets the MFN rate at 6.4%, Regulation 978/2012 determines that Indonesia gets no GSP preference for this section (graduated), and TARIC applies it all in the daily customs system.
